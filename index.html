Abstract

This research project investigates how different vision fields affect predator-prey interactions. By simulating simplified environments and training agents with reinforcement learning, we hope to identify trends that emerge in the strategies and effectiveness of trained predator and prey agents which use varying vision fields.

We also hope to examine the psychology of chasing and how prey can use occlusions in the environment to their advantage. Our findings can help deepen our understanding of why various animals have evolved to have different vision systems.

Related work

There isn't much existing work related to simulating predator prey dynamics, especially in the context of vision. The two closest studies we found are described below.


Co-Evolution of Predator-Prey Ecosystems by Reinforcement Learning Agents [1]

This study utilises multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) approaches to simulate co-evolution mechanisms in predator-prey ecosystems, in which agents show a biologically plausible approximation of their co-evolution over multiple generations in nature.

Left: Initial random location of predators and prey.
Right: Emergence of swarming among predators and prey.

This work however only uses a very simplified 2D environment without vision.

Emergent tool use from multi-agent autocurricula [2]

This work by OpenAI explores how agents in a hide-and-seek game environment (which is similar in some ways to predator-prey interactions) develop sophisticated tool use and coordination. The study provides evidence that multi-agent self-play can lead to emergent auto curricula.

Use of vision sensors in the hide-and-seek environment

Whlist the agents are trained in a very complex environment with vision sensors, this study doesn't examine the effects of varying the vision fields on the emergent behaviours by the agents.


Our approach differs from these studies in the sense that we emphasize the emergence of different behaviours based on visual perception of the agents, in terms of field of view and depth perception.
